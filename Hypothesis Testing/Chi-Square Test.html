Chi-Square Test \((\chi^2)\)

यह बहुत important है क्योंकि:

  • Z-Test → Means के लिए
  • t-Test → Means के लिए
  • Chi-Square Test → Categories / Counts / Frequencies के लिए

सबसे पहले समझो: Chi-Square कब use होता है?

अब तक हम पूछते थे:

Average marks कितने हैं?

Average salary कितनी है?

यानी Mean पर काम कर रहे थे।

Chi-Square में हम पूछते हैं:

कितने लोग Male हैं और कितने Female?

कितनों ने Product A चुना और कितनों ने Product B?

Gender और Product Choice related हैं या नहीं?

यानी counts (गिनती) पर काम करते हैं।

Example

100 लोगों से पूछा:

Product People
A 40
B 35
C 25

अब सवाल:

क्या यह distribution expected distribution से match करता है?

यहीं Chi-Square आता है।

Chi-Square Formula

पूरे chapter की foundation:

जहाँ:

  • O = Observed Frequency
  • E = Expected Frequency

Meaning

Observed = जो वास्तव में मिला

Expected = जो \(H_0\) के अनुसार मिलना चाहिए था

Example

मान लो coin fair है।

100 toss किए।

Expected:

Outcome Expected
Head 50
Tail 50

लेकिन मिला:

Outcome Observed
Head 60
Tail 40

अब हम पूछते हैं:

क्या 60-40 सिर्फ chance की वजह से है?

या

Coin biased हो सकता है?

यही Chi-Square का काम है।

1. Goodness of Fit Test — Complete Numerical Example

Situation

एक dice manufacturer दावा करता है:

Dice fair है।

यदि dice fair है, तो 6 faces के आने की probability बराबर होनी चाहिए।

\[P(1) = P(2) = P(3) = P(4) = P(5) = P(6) = \frac{1}{6}\]

Step 1: Collect Data

Dice को 60 बार roll किया गया।

Observed Frequencies:

Face Observed (O)
1 5
2 8
3 9
4 12
5 14
6 12

Total:

\[5+8+9+12+14+12=60\]

Step 2: Hypotheses

Null Hypothesis

\[H_0 : Dice\ is\ fair\]

या

\[P(1) = P(2) = P(3) = P(4) = P(5) = P(6) = \frac{1}{6}\]

Alternative Hypothesis

\[H_1 : Dice\ is\ not\ fair\]

Step 3: Expected Frequencies

यहीं सबसे important concept है।

Question

अगर dice fair है तो 60 rolls में 1 कितनी बार आना चाहिए?

Probability:

\[\frac{1}{6}\]

Total Rolls:

\[60\]

Expected Frequency:

\[E = n \times p\]
\[E = 60 \times \frac{1}{6}\]
\[E = 10\]

इसी तरह सभी faces के लिए:

Face Expected (E)
1 10
2 10
3 10
4 10
5 10
6 10

Full Table

Face O E
1 5 10
2 8 10
3 9 10
4 12 10
5 14 10
6 12 10

Step 4: Chi-Square Formula

अब हर row के लिए calculate करेंगे।

Face O E O−E (O−E)² (O−E)²/E
1 5 10 -5 25 2.5
2 8 10 -2 4 0.4
3 9 10 -1 1 0.1
4 12 10 2 4 0.4
5 14 10 4 16 1.6
6 12 10 2 4 0.4

अब last column जोड़ो:

\[2.5+0.4+0.1+0.4+1.6+0.4\]
\[\chi^2 = 5.4\]

✅ Chi-Square Statistic = 5.4

Formula :

\[\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O-E)^2}{E}\]

Step 6: Degrees of Freedom

Goodness of Fit Test में:

\[df = k - 1\]

जहाँ:

\[k=Number\ of\ Categories\]

यहाँ:

\[k = 6\]

तो:

\[df = k - 1\]
\[df = 6 - 1\]
\[\boxed{df = 5}\]

✅ df = 5

Step 7: Critical Value

Depends on:

  • \(\alpha\)
  • df

यहाँ:

\[\alpha = 0.05\]
\[df = 5\]

Critical Value:

\[Critical\ Value = 11.07\]

Step 8: Decision

Compare:

\[\chi^2 = 5.4\]
\[Critical = 11.07\]
\[5.4 < 11.07\]

✅ \(H_0\) Not Rejected

Step 9: p-value Decision

Depends on:

  • \(\chi^2\)
  • df

इसलिए

\[P-Value = 0.999971\]

और

\[0.999971 > 0.05\]

✅ \(H_0\) Not Rejected

Final Conclusion

There is insufficient evidence to conclude that the dice is biased.

या

उपलब्ध data यह साबित नहीं करता कि dice unfair है।

या

Dice को fair मानने वाले claim को reject करने के लिए पर्याप्त evidence नहीं है।

Goodness of Fit Test — Company Survey Example

Situation

एक e-commerce company दावा करती है कि उसके customers चार product categories में इस proportion से खरीदारी करते हैं:

Category Expected Share
Electronics 40%
Clothing 30%
Home Decor 20%
Books 10%

Company ने 200 recent orders का sample लिया।

Step 1: Hypotheses

\[H_0 :\]

Customer purchases expected distribution follow करते हैं।

\[H_1 :\]

Customer purchases expected distribution follow नहीं करते।

Step 2: Observed Data

Category Observed (O)
Electronics 90
Clothing 50
Home Decor 40
Books 20

Total:

\[200\]

Step 3: Expected Frequencies

Formula :

\[E = n \times p\]

Electronics

\[E = 200 \times 0.40\]
\[E = 80\]

Clothing

\[E = 200 \times 0.30\]
\[E = 60\]

Home Decor

\[E = 200 \times 0.20\]
\[E = 40\]

Books

\[E = 200 \times 0.10\]
E = 20

Expected Table

Category O E
Electronics 90 80
Clothing 50 60
Home Decor 40 40
Books 20 20

Step 4: Chi-Square Calculation

\[\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O-E)^2}{E}\]
Category O E O−E (O−E)²/E
Electronics 90 80 10 1.25
Clothing 50 60 -10 1.67
Home Decor 40 40 0 0
Books 20 20 0 0
\[\chi^2 = 1.25+1.67\]
\[\chi^2 = 2.92\]

Step 5: Degrees of Freedom

Categories:

\[k = 4\]
\[df = k - 1\]
\[df = 4 - 1\]
\[df = 3\]

Step 6: Critical Value

\[\alpha = 0.05\]
\[df = 3\]

Critical Value:

\[7.815\]

Step 7: Decision

\[2.92 < 7.815\]

✅ \(H_0\) Not Rejected

Step 8: p-value Decision

\[\chi^2 = 2.92\]
\[df = 3\]

P-Value :

\[0.40\]

Compare:

\[\alpha = 0.05\]
\[0.40 > 0.05\]

✅ \(H_0\) Not Rejected

Final Conclusion

There is insufficient evidence to conclude that customer purchases differ from the company's claimed distribution.

या

Sample data company के claimed customer purchase distribution के खिलाफ पर्याप्त evidence नहीं देता।

Test of Independence — Complete Numerical Example

Situation

एक company जानना चाहती है:

क्या Gender और Product Choice के बीच कोई relationship है?

100 customers का survey किया गया।

Step 1: Observed Data

Gender Product A Product B Row Total
Male 30 20 50
Female 10 40 50
Column Total 40 60 100

यह table Observed Frequencies (O) की table है।

Step 2: Hypotheses

Null Hypothesis

\[H_0 :\]

Gender and Product Choice are independent.

Alternative Hypothesis

\[H_1\]

Gender and Product Choice are not independent.

Step 3: Expected Frequencies

यहाँ सबसे important नया concept आता है।

Formula:

\[E = \frac{(Row\ Total) (Column\ Total)}{Grand\ Total}\]

Cell 1: Male & Product A

Observed:

\[O = 30\]

Expected:

\[E = \frac{50 \times 40}{100}\]
\[E = 20\]

Cell 2: Male & Product B

\[E = \frac{50 \times 60}{100}\]
\[E = 30\]

Cell 3: Female & Product A

\[E = \frac{50 \times 40}{100}\]
\[E = 20\]

Cell 4: Female & Product B

\[E = \frac{50 \times 60}{100}\]
\[E = 30\]

Expected Frequency Table

Gender Product A Product B
Male 20 30
Female 20 30

Step 4: Why Did We Get These Expected Values?

यह सबसे important understanding है।

अगर Gender और Product Choice वास्तव में independent हैं:

तो Product A के 40 customers हैं।

100 customers में से:

\[40\%\]

ने Product A चुना।

Male customers:

\[50\]

हैं।

यदि independence सही है:

तो Male group में भी लगभग

\[40\%\]

को Product A चुनना चाहिए।

\[50 \times 0.40 = 20\]

इसलिए Expected Frequency = 20 आती है।

Step 5: Chi-Square Calculation Table

Formula:

Cell O E O−E (O−E)²/E
Male-A 30 20 10 5
Male-B 20 30 -10 3.33
Female-A 10 20 -10 5
Female-B 40 30 10 3.33

Add last column:

\[5+3.33+5+3.33\]
\[\chi^2 = 16.66\]

✅ Chi-Square Statistic = 16.66

Step 6: Degrees of Freedom

Test of Independence में:

\[df = (r - 1) (c - 1)\]

जहाँ:

  • r = row
  • c = column

यहाँ

\[r = 2\]
\[c = 2\]
\[df = (2 - 1)(2 - 1)\]
\[df = 1\]

Step 7: Critical Value Decision

Depends on:

  • \(\alpha\)
  • df
\[\alpha = 0.05\]
\[df = 1\]

Critical Value:

\[3.841\]

Compare:

\[16.66 > 3.841\]

✅ Reject \(H_0\)

Step 8: p-value Decision

Depends on:

  • \(\chi^2\)
  • df
\[\chi^2 = 16.66\]
\[df = 1\]

p-value:

\[P-Value = 0.000045\]

Compare :

\[0.000045 < 0.05\]

✅ Reject \(H_0\)

Final Conclusion

There is sufficient evidence to conclude that Gender and Product Choice are associated.

या

Gender और Product Choice independent नहीं हैं।

या

Customer gender product choice को influence करता प्रतीत होता है।

Test of Homogeneity — Complete Numerical Example

Situation

एक company जानना चाहती है:

क्या Nagpur, Pune और Mumbai में product preference का distribution समान है?

300 customers का survey किया गया।

Step 1: Observed Data

City Product A Product B Row Total
Nagpur 40 60 100
Pune 50 50 100
Mumbai 70 30 100
Column Total 160 140 300

Step 2: Hypotheses

Null Hypothesis

\[H_0 :\]

सभी cities में product preference का distribution समान है।

Alternative Hypothesis

\[H_1 :\]

सभी cities में product preference का distribution समान नहीं है।

Step 3: Expected Frequencies

Formula:

\[E = \frac{(Row\ Total)(Column\ Total)}{Grand\ Total}\]

Nagpur - Product A

\[E = \frac{100 \times 160}{300}\]
\[\boxed{E = 53.33}\]

Nagpur - Product B

\[E = \frac{100 \times 140}{300}\]
\[\boxed{E = 46.67}\]

क्योंकि Pune और Mumbai की row totals भी 100 हैं, इसलिए Expected Frequencies भी वही आएँगी।

Expected Table

City Product A Product B
Nagpur 53.33 46.67
Pune 53.33 46.67
Mumbai 53.33 46.67

Step 4: Chi-Square Calculation

Formula:

Cell O E (O−E)²/E
Nagpur-A 40 53.33 3.33
Nagpur-B 60 46.67 3.81
Pune-A 50 53.33 0.21
Pune-B 50 46.67 0.24
Mumbai-A 70 53.33 5.21
Mumbai-B 30 46.67 5.95

अब सब जोड़ते हैं:

\[\chi^2 = 3.33+3.81+0.21+0.24+5.21+5.95\]
\[\chi^2 = 18.75\]

✅ Chi-Square Statistic = 18.75

Step 5: Degrees of Freedom

Formula:

\[df=(r−1)(c−1)\]

यहाँ:

\[r = 3\]
\[c = 2\]
\[df = (3 - 1) (2 - 1)\]
\[\boxed{df = 2}\]

✅ df = 2

Step 6: Critical Value Decision

\[\alpha = 0.05\]
\[df = 2\]

Critical Value:

\[5.991\]

Compare:

\[18.75>5.991\]

✅ Reject \(H_0\)

Step 7: p-value Decision

Depends on:

  • \(\chi^2 = 18.75\)
  • df = 2

p-value:

\[0.0000928\]

Compare:

\[0.0000928 < 0.05\]

✅ Reject \(H_0\)

Final Conclusion

There is sufficient evidence to conclude that product preference distributions are not the same across Nagpur, Pune, and Mumbai.

तीनों cities में customers की product preferences समान नहीं हैं।
